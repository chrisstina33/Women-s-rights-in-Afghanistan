The Situation of Women in Afghanistan

Since the Taliban returned to power in August 2021, the situation of women in Afghanistan has significantly worsened, being described by the UN as one of the most repressive in the world.

Education and Work

Girls over the age of 12 have been banned from attending schools and universities. Women are no longer allowed to study in fields such as medicine, dentistry, or nursing. Access to higher education is completely forbidden, which also negatively impacts the healthcare system, where women in some provinces can no longer be treated by male doctors.

Additionally, most women have been prevented from returning to work, including in schools and universities. The Taliban do not support the involvement of women in leadership roles and have used lethal force to disperse protests in favor of women’s rights.

Social and Mobility Restrictions

Women are not allowed to travel more than 72 kilometers (about 45 miles) without being accompanied by a close male relative. They are also banned from accessing parks, gyms, public baths, and beauty salons. The dress code requires them to wear a burqa or hijab, and women are not allowed to speak loudly in public or be heard from outside their homes.

Oppression and Violence

The Taliban have abolished legal aid programs and special courts that were designed to fight violence against women and girls. In extreme cases, women have been subjected to sexual violence and torture in prisons. The number of suicides among women has dramatically increased, indicating a severe mental health crisis.

The Voices of Afghan Women

Zarqa Yaftali, the director of a women’s foundation in Afghanistan, stated: “Afghan women are the ones the world forgets. They are a marginalized group in the country and are the victims of political games. They have no support; right now, they are fighting alone.”

Despite these restrictions, some women continue to resist by organizing protests and launching underground initiatives to support education and their fundamental rights.

For more information, you can consult the Wikipedia page on Women in Afghanistan.

Our Project

This project works with non-governmental organizations that are building educational institutions for Afghan girls and women. These institutions offer scholarships, meals, and educational resources such as books, tablets, laptops, and smartphones. Since education for girls is forbidden in many areas, the use of these items must remain secret to protect them from government retaliation.
